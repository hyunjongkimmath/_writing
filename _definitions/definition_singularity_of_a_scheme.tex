\begin{definition} \label{definition:singularity_of_a_scheme}
Let $X$ be a variety or scheme over $k$. A \hldef{singularity of $X$} is a point $x \in X$ where the local ring $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ fails to be regular. Important classes of singularities include:  
\begin{itemize}
\item \hldef{normal crossings singularity}: locally Ã©tale isomorphic to $\Spec k[x_1,\dots,x_n]/(x_1 \cdots x_r)$ for some $r \leq n$,
\item \hldef{rational singularity}: normal singularity $x \in X$ such that $R^i \pi_* \mathcal{O}_Y = 0$ for $i > 0$ for a resolution $\pi: Y \to X$,
\item \hldef{canonical singularity}: a normal singularity $x \in X$ with discrepancy $\geq 0$ with respect to the canonical divisor $K_X$,
\item \hldef{log canonical singularity}: a pair $(X,D)$ where $D$ is an effective divisor, with discrepancy $\geq -1$,
\item \hldef{log terminal singularity}: a stronger condition with discrepancy $> -1$.
\end{itemize}  
\end{definition}
