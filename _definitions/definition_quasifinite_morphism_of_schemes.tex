\begin{definition}[Quasifinite morphism] \label{definition:quasifinite_morphism_of_schemes}
Let $f \colon X \to Y$ be a \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:morphism_of_schemes}{morphism of schemes}. 
We say that $f$ is \hldef{quasifinite} if $f$ is of \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:finite_type_morphism_of_schemes}{finite type} and has finite fibers, meaning that for every point $y \in Y$, the fiber $X_y = X \times_Y \operatorname{Spec} \kappa(y)$\CrefIfExists{definition:residue_field_of_a_scheme_at_a_point}\CrefIfExists{definition:affine_scheme} is a finite scheme (i.e., $X_y$ is a scheme with finitely many points and $\Gamma(X_y, \mathcal{O}_{X_y})$ is a \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:basis_of_a_vector_space_over_a_field}{finite-dimensional} \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:algebra_over_a_sheaf_of_rings_on_a_site}{$\kappa(y)$-algebra}).
\end{definition}
