\begin{definition} \label{definition:algebraic_curve_over_a_field}
Let $k$ be a field. 
An \hldef{algebraic curve over $k$} is a \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:scheme}{scheme} $C$ that is \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:quasi_separated_morphism_of_schemes}{separated}, of \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:finite_type_morphism_of_schemes}{finite type} over $k$, and purely of dimension 1.
\TODO{define pure dimension of a scheme}

A curve is called \hldef{complete} if it is \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:proper_morphism_of_schemes}{proper} over $k$. Many writers will typically assume algebraic curves to be complete and \CrefAndHyperrefIfExist{definition:geometrically_connected_scheme_over_a_field}{geometrically connected}.

% Typically, unless stated otherwise, we assume a curve is also \hldef{complete} (proper over \hl{$k$}) and \hldef{geometrically connected}.
\end{definition}
